Antimicrobial Resistance (AMR) and Dentistry

Abstract

In the unfolding narrative of global health, the ominous spectre of antimicrobial resistance (AMR) looms large, presenting an ever more urgent challenge to public health systems worldwide. Antibiotics are a primary tool in the field of therapeutic medicine & dentistry & they saturate the Indian pharmaceutical markets. Dentistry places substantial reliance on use of antibiotics in prophylaxis & therapy, but this dependence contributes to the overuse & misuse of these drugs. A significant number of prescriptions are frequently deemed superfluous. Furthermore, antibiotics are readily accessible over the counter in addition to being subject to notable instances of overprescription. The absence of timely antibiotic culture & susceptibility tools accelerates the dissemination of resistance, resembling a wildfire.

Given current trends, it appears we are unwittingly fostering a situation akin to Frankenstein's monster. But is this foresight or mere paranoia? Our focus must centre not only on bolstering surveillance but also on the understanding of the need of antibiotics in dental treatment, advocating for responsible antibiotic usage & advancing research into innovative treatments, in return fortifying healthcare systems. Failure to curb the spread of antimicrobial resistance, at best- jeopardises individual health to a point of no return, & at its worst- undermines the sustainability of modern medicine as we know it. This article is aimed to depict the contemporary landscape of antimicrobial resistance within the realm of dentistry ,speculate on its potential detrimental trajectory.

1. Introduction

Antimicrobials are a group of medications that either eradicate or restrain the growth of microorganisms. This category includes antibiotics, antivirals, antifungals, and antiparasitics. They are essential drugs used to combat infectious diseases in humans, animals, and plants.Antimicrobial Resistance (AMR) arises when microorganisms like bacteria, viruses, fungi, and parasites develop insensitivity to these medicines. This renders antimicrobial drugs ineffective, making their infections challenging or impossible to treat. Consequently, there's an elevated risk of disease transmission, severe illness, morbidity and mortality.(1)

Upon the introduction of antimicrobials, most bacteria are eradicated, yet some persist. Development of resistance is an inherent process that unfolds gradually due to genetic shifts in pathogens.Through repeated cycles of exposure to antimicrobials, only the fittest survive, leading to the development of highly resistant strains. Human actions, especially the overuse and abuse of antimicrobials for treating, preventing, or controlling infections , hence, accelerate the emergence and spread of AMR.(1)

2. Evolution and Identification of resistant bacteria

AMR is one of the WHOs major global health concerns(1). Over time, bacteria have evolved various adaptive mechanisms to resist antibiotics. The primary methods of AMR include enzymatic inactivation of antimicrobial compounds, alteration of a drug target, reduced permeability of the outer membrane, and active drug efflux (13).Hydrolases, passivation, and modified enzymes are among the critical drug-inactivating enzymes. The modification of target sites is a significant factor in the development of drug resistance in Gram-positive bacteria, such as gene PBP2a in methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA), MRSA by the acquisition of the mecA gene and similar variants, as well as in polymyxin-resistant bacterial strains.Altering the bacterial envelope by reducing porin production or enhancing the expression of efflux pump systems has also been reported(14). Current testing modalities include the contemporary methods like broth dilution, agar dilution, disk diffusion and newer methods like chromogenic media and genomic methods. Their disadvantage being common- they all are time consuming methods. Some other methods include automated systems for susceptibility testing, genetics methods and Matrix-assisted laser desorption ionisation-time of flight mass spectrometry (MALDI-TOFMS) but being either highly technical or high cost procedure they can not be used routinely(13). The PA-100 AST System by Sysmex Astrego has recently won the Longitude Prize for its advanced point-of-care test for Urinary Tract Infections (UTIs). This technology swiftly identifies the UTI-causing bacteria and conducts antibiotic susceptibility testing in less than 45 minutes, aiding in personalised antibiotic selection.(30) This provides promising prospects for future advancements and the application of comparable technology to combat antibiotic resistance in the oral environment.

3. Present situation and statistics

According to the World Health Organization (WHO) in 2019, AMR was directly responsible for a staggering 1.27 million deaths, disproportionately impacting socioeconomically vulnerable regions.About 410,000 children under five years old die from pneumonia in India each year, representing nearly a quarter of all child deaths. The current crude mortality rate from infectious diseases in India is 417 per 100,000 people, indicating a potentially greater impact of AMR in this context(18).

Recently, Global AMR and Use Surveillance System (GLASS) report 2022 indicate a concerning trend, with 76 countries exhibiting significant rates of resistance, particularly evident in third-generation cephalosporin-resistant E. coli (42%) and methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (35%) .Klebsiella pneumoniae, a frequently found bacterium in the intestines, has demonstrated higher resistance to essential antibiotics. This resistance escalation may necessitate the increased use of last-resort medications such as carbapenems, resistance to which has also started spreading across various areas.(1) This is often referred to as 'Resistome' which is the total compilation of antibiotic resistance genes (ARGs) and their precursor. It includes genes from both harmful and harmless bacteria, like the ones that cause diseases, the ones that produce antibiotics themselves, and the ones that don't harm us. It is present in the living beings and the ecosphere beyond the living body- the land, air and water.(2)

In the oral ecosystem it has emerged as the oral resistome. A recent research project used shotgun metagenomics to analyse oral and gut samples collected from healthy individuals across various countries. They discovered variations in the prevalence of antibiotic resistance genes (ARGs) both among countries and between oral and stool samples. Specifically, they observed that oral samples had a higher abundance but lower diversity of ARGs compared to gut samples(3). Forces us to think if we are treading the path of resistance just a few steps behind Klebsiella pneumoniae.It isn't a trend limited to bacteria , drug resistant fungi and viruses have also seen a surge recently, specially in the post COVID-19 era.

Where may it lead us? According to forecasts from the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD), there is an expected doubling in resistance to last-resort antibiotics by 2035 compared to levels seen in 2005. This highlights the pressing necessity for strong antimicrobial stewardship measures and improved surveillance globally.(1).

4. Mechanisms of Antibiotic Resistance

The molecular mechanisms underlying antibiotic resistance are diverse and sophisticated. Bacteria employ multiple strategies to evade the action of antimicrobial agents. Enzymatic inactivation represents one of the most common mechanisms, where bacteria produce enzymes that chemically modify or destroy antibiotics. Beta-lactamases, for instance, hydrolyze the beta-lactam ring of penicillins and cephalosporins, rendering them ineffective. More recently, extended-spectrum beta-lactamases (ESBLs) and carbapenemases have emerged, capable of inactivating even broader classes of antibiotics, creating significant treatment challenges in clinical settings worldwide.(15)

Target modification constitutes another crucial resistance mechanism. Bacteria can alter the molecular targets of antibiotics through genetic mutations or acquisition of resistant genes. For example, mutations in DNA gyrase and topoisomerase IV reduce binding affinity for fluoroquinolones, while modifications in ribosomal RNA confer resistance to macrolides, tetracyclines, and aminoglycosides. The acquisition of the mecA gene in Staphylococcus aureus leads to production of penicillin-binding protein 2a (PBP2a), which has low affinity for beta-lactam antibiotics, resulting in methicillin resistance (MRSA). This genetic plasticity enables rapid adaptation to antimicrobial pressure and facilitates the emergence of multidrug-resistant strains.(16)

5. Global Impact and Economic Consequences

The global impact of antimicrobial resistance extends far beyond clinical settings, affecting economies, food security, and modern medical practice. The World Bank estimates that AMR could cause global economic damage comparable to the 2008 financial crisis, with a reduction of 1.1-3.8% in global GDP by 2050 if left unaddressed. Low and middle-income countries would bear the brunt of this impact, potentially pushing an additional 28 million people into poverty, primarily due to increased healthcare costs, reduced labor productivity, and elevated mortality rates across all age groups.(19)

In healthcare systems worldwide, AMR threatens the foundation of modern medicine. Procedures that rely on effective antibiotic prophylaxis—including organ transplantation, cancer chemotherapy, joint replacements, and caesarean sections—become significantly riskier in a post-antibiotic era. The rise of resistant pathogens increases hospital stays, requires more expensive second- and third-line treatments, and escalates healthcare costs substantially. Studies indicate that infections caused by resistant bacteria are associated with approximately double the mortality rate, longer hospital stays, and increased healthcare costs compared to susceptible infections, creating unsustainable burdens on healthcare infrastructures globally.(20)

6. Strategies for Containment and Future Directions

Addressing the AMR crisis requires a multifaceted One Health approach that recognizes the interconnectedness of human health, animal health, and the environment. Key strategies include strengthening surveillance systems through initiatives like the Global Antimicrobial Resistance and Use Surveillance System (GLASS), which coordinates standardized data collection across countries. Enhanced laboratory capacity, particularly in low-resource settings, is essential for accurate diagnosis and monitoring of resistance patterns. The development and implementation of rapid diagnostic tests can facilitate targeted therapy and reduce unnecessary antibiotic use, thereby decreasing selective pressure for resistance development.(21)

Antibiotic stewardship programs represent a cornerstone of containment efforts. These programs promote appropriate antibiotic selection, dosing, route, and duration of therapy across healthcare settings. Educational initiatives targeting both prescribers and the public are crucial for changing behaviors and perceptions regarding antibiotic use. Simultaneously, infection prevention and control measures—including hand hygiene, sanitation, vaccination, and hospital accreditation programs—reduce the transmission of resistant organisms. Investment in research and development for novel antibiotics, alternative therapies, and vaccines remains imperative, as the pipeline for new antimicrobial agents has been largely stagnant for decades despite the growing threat of resistance.(22)

7. The Role of Pharmaceutical Pollution and Environmental Transmission

Pharmaceutical manufacturing facilities and improper disposal of medicines contribute significantly to environmental contamination with antibiotics, creating selective pressure for resistance development in natural bacterial populations. Wastewater treatment plants often fail to completely remove antibiotic residues, which then enter rivers, lakes, and agricultural systems. This environmental contamination facilitates the horizontal gene transfer of resistance determinants between environmental bacteria and human pathogens, effectively turning natural ecosystems into reservoirs and breeding grounds for resistance genes.(23)

The spread of antibiotic resistance genes through environmental pathways represents a critical challenge for containment efforts. Studies have detected clinically relevant resistance genes in diverse environments, including soil, water, and wildlife, often far removed from direct human antibiotic use. The resistome—the complete collection of resistance genes and their precursors in pathogenic and non-pathogenic bacteria—demonstrates remarkable mobility across ecosystem boundaries. This environmental dimension of AMR necessitates integrated approaches that address pollution control, waste management, and regulatory frameworks governing antibiotic manufacturing and disposal to effectively curb the amplification and dissemination of resistance in the environment.(24)

8. Diagnostic Innovations and Precision Medicine Approaches

Recent advances in diagnostic technologies offer promising avenues for combating antimicrobial resistance. Next-generation sequencing platforms enable rapid identification of pathogens and their resistance profiles directly from clinical samples, potentially reducing turnaround times from days to hours. Metagenomic approaches can detect resistance genes without prior culture, providing comprehensive insights into the resistome of clinical samples. These technologies facilitate earlier targeted therapy and enhance surveillance capabilities for emerging resistance threats, though challenges related to cost, standardization, and data interpretation remain barriers to widespread implementation in routine clinical practice.(25)

Point-of-care testing devices represent another frontier in the fight against AMR. Microfluidic systems, biosensors, and paper-based assays are being developed to provide rapid, affordable resistance detection in resource-limited settings. The integration of artificial intelligence and machine learning algorithms with diagnostic data can further enhance pattern recognition, predict resistance based on genomic markers, and guide therapeutic decisions. These precision medicine approaches aim to move beyond empirical antibiotic prescribing toward tailored therapies based on individual pathogen profiles, potentially reducing unnecessary antibiotic exposure and slowing the development of resistance while improving patient outcomes.(26)

9. Antimicrobial Resistance in the Post-COVID-19 Era

The COVID-19 pandemic has profoundly impacted the trajectory of antimicrobial resistance worldwide. During the initial phases of the pandemic, extensive empirical antibiotic use in COVID-19 patients—despite low rates of bacterial co-infection—created unprecedented selective pressure for resistance development. Studies indicate that approximately 70-80% of hospitalized COVID-19 patients received antibiotics, often broad-spectrum agents, while bacterial co-infection rates were estimated at only 8-12%. This massive antibiotic pressure, combined with disruptions to stewardship programs and infection control measures, has likely accelerated the emergence and spread of resistant pathogens in healthcare settings globally.(27)

Healthcare system disruptions during the pandemic have had complex effects on AMR patterns. While reduced elective procedures and international travel initially decreased some healthcare-associated infections, overwhelmed hospitals experienced outbreaks of difficult-to-treat resistant organisms. The redirection of public health resources away from AMR surveillance and containment efforts has created significant data gaps in many regions. Additionally, the increased use of telemedicine and self-medication practices during lockdowns may have influenced antibiotic prescribing patterns in community settings. Understanding these pandemic-related impacts is crucial for recalibrating AMR containment strategies in the post-COVID-19 era and rebuilding resilient health systems capable of addressing both pandemic threats and the silent pandemic of antimicrobial resistance.(28)

10. Conclusion and Future Perspectives

Antimicrobial resistance represents one of the most complex public health challenges of our time, requiring coordinated global action across multiple sectors. The silent pandemic of AMR continues to escalate, with current estimates suggesting it could cause 10 million deaths annually by 2050 if adequate containment measures are not implemented. The economic consequences similarly threaten to reverse decades of developmental progress, particularly in low and middle-income countries. While the challenges are formidable, the growing recognition of AMR as a global health security priority has stimulated unprecedented political commitment and multisectoral collaboration through frameworks like the Global Action Plan on Antimicrobial Resistance and national action plans in many countries.(29)

The path forward demands sustained investment in research and innovation, strengthened health systems, and the integration of AMR considerations across all relevant policy domains. Promising developments include novel antibacterial approaches such as phage therapy, monoclonal antibodies, and immunomodulatory agents that may complement or replace traditional antibiotics. Advances in vaccine development could prevent infections and reduce antibiotic use, while digital health technologies offer new tools for surveillance, diagnosis, and stewardship. Ultimately, preserving the effectiveness of antimicrobial agents requires a fundamental shift in how these precious resources are valued, regulated, and used across human health, animal health, and environmental sectors to ensure their availability for future generations.(30)

Antibiotic Usage Tips

1

Always complete the full antibiotic course

Even if symptoms improve early, stopping your antibiotic too soon allows surviving bacteria to multiply again, increasing the risk of resistance and treatment failure.

2

Never use antibiotics without a confirmed medical diagnosis

Antibiotics should only be taken when prescribed by a qualified healthcare professional after proper clinical assessment.

3

Do not use antibiotics for viral infections

Colds, flu, most sore throats, bronchitis, or viral fever do not improve with antibiotics, and taking them unnecessarily contributes to resistance.

4

Never share antibiotics with others

Each antibiotic is prescribed based on specific bacteria, patient age, weight, allergies, and medical history. Sharing them can be dangerous and ineffective.

5

Avoid taking leftover antibiotics

Leftover medications may not match the infection you currently have, may be expired, and can lead to incomplete treatment.

6

Follow the exact dosing schedule

Skipping doses or taking them at the wrong time lowers antibiotic levels in your body, giving bacteria a chance to survive and adapt.

7

Do not pressure the doctor for antibiotics

Trust your healthcare provider’s judgment. If they say you don’t need antibiotics, it’s because the infection is not bacterial.

8

Report any allergic reactions immediately

Rashes, swelling, difficulty breathing, or severe gastrointestinal symptoms may indicate an allergy and require urgent medical attention.

9

Avoid mixing antibiotics with alcohol

Alcohol may interfere with the effectiveness of certain antibiotics and can worsen side effects like nausea and dizziness.

10

Take antibiotics with or without food as directed

Some antibiotics require an empty stomach, while others must be taken with meals to ensure proper absorption.

11

Store antibiotics properly

Heat, direct sunlight, and humidity can reduce effectiveness. Follow storage instructions carefully.

12

Never use antibiotics as a preventive measure unless prescribed

Preventive antibiotics are only needed in specific situations, such as surgeries or dental procedures.

13

Inform your doctor about other medications

Some antibiotics interact with blood thinners, birth control pills, antacids, and other prescriptions.

14

Do not double the dose if you miss one

If you forget a dose, take it as soon as you remember unless it is close to the next dose.

15

Keep a record of your antibiotic history

Patients who frequently use antibiotics may need alternative treatments due to prior resistance patterns.

16

Avoid self-diagnosing bacterial infections

Only medical evaluation can determine whether an infection is truly bacterial.

17

Drink plenty of water during treatment

Hydration helps your body process the antibiotic and reduces kidney-related side effects.

18

Inform your doctor if symptoms do not improve

You may need a different antibiotic or further evaluation if symptoms persist.

19

Stronger antibiotics are not always better

Antibiotics are chosen based on bacteria type, not “strength.”

20

Be cautious when using antibiotics in children

Children require weight-based dosing and careful monitoring.

21

Avoid using old medical prescriptions

An antibiotic that worked previously may not be correct for your current infection.

22

Maintain good hygiene to reduce infections

Clean hands reduce the need for antibiotics by preventing infections.

23

Use probiotics if recommended

They help restore gut bacteria and reduce diarrhea caused by antibiotics.

24

Respect the time interval between doses

Even spacing ensures stable antibiotic levels in your bloodstream.

25

Do not crush or split antibiotics unless allowed

Some tablets must remain whole for safe and effective absorption.

26

Avoid antibiotics for mild ear infections

Many ear infections resolve on their own without antibiotics.

27

Learn the difference between side effects and allergies

Mild stomach upset is common; swelling or breathing difficulty is dangerous.

28

Never use veterinary antibiotics

Animal antibiotics are unsafe and inappropriate for humans.

29

Dispose of unused antibiotics safely

Use pharmacy take-back programs to prevent misuse.

30

Avoid sharing medical advice about antibiotics

Each infection is unique. Encourage others to see a doctor.

31

Be honest with your doctor about symptoms

Incomplete information may lead to the wrong antibiotic.

32

Take note of foods that interact with antibiotics

Dairy can reduce absorption of tetracyclines and similar medications.

33

Understand that antibiotic resistance affects everyone

Misuse leads to infections that no longer respond to treatment.

34

Pregnant and breastfeeding women must consult doctors

Certain antibiotics are unsafe during pregnancy and may affect the baby.

35

Avoid unnecessary broad-spectrum antibiotics

They kill good bacteria and increase resistance.

36

Check your temperature regularly during treatment

This helps determine whether the treatment is working properly.

37

Store liquid antibiotics in the refrigerator if required

Some suspensions need refrigeration to stay effective.

38

Finish antibiotics even if tests later show improvement

Stopping early allows remaining bacteria to survive and mutate.

39

Avoid taking antibiotics for dental pain without diagnosis

Most dental pain is inflammatory, not bacterial.

40

Educate family and friends about proper antibiotic use

Raising awareness helps reduce unnecessary prescriptions.
